History

75 Years of PH–Indonesia Friendship — A Filipino's Tour

Filipinos in Bali•Published May 26, 2026

Hero photo: Gunawan Kartapranata, CC BY-SA 3.0. Illustrative image; not a photo of BaliFILs members.

When you live in Bali, you're a small part of a long story. The Philippines and Indonesia have been close neighbours for as long as either has been a country — and well before. In 2024 the two nations celebrated 75 years of formal diplomatic relations. Here is the story behind that milestone, told for kababayans who get to live inside it.

Before there were countries

The Philippines and Indonesia share the deepest possible root: both archipelagos were settled by Austronesian peoples moving south and east about 4,000 years ago. The languages, the rice culture, the way villages organise themselves, the wood and bamboo architecture, the importance of family — these are common inheritances. Filipinos and Indonesians are not just neighbours. They are cousins.

1949 — diplomatic relations established

On November 24, 1949, the newly independent Republic of the Philippines and the newly independent Republic of Indonesia formally established diplomatic relations. The Philippines had gained independence from the United States in 1946; Indonesia would have its own independence formally recognised by the Netherlands in December 1949. The two young nations recognised each other immediately.

1951 — the Treaty of Friendship

On June 21, 1951, in Jakarta, the two countries signed the Treaty of Friendship between the Philippines and Indonesia. It was one of the earliest post-independence friendship treaties in Southeast Asia. It is the legal door every kababayan walks through to live and work here today.

ASEAN — co-founders, 1967

In 1967, the Philippines and Indonesia, together with Malaysia, Singapore, and Thailand, founded the Association of Southeast Asian Nations (ASEAN). From that day forward, the two countries' political and economic destinies have been formally intertwined. ASEAN membership is also what gives Filipino passport holders visa-free entry to Indonesia for short visits.

People-to-people: the long quiet thread

The diplomatic timeline only tells part of the story. Underneath, Filipino people have been moving to Indonesia for decades:

  • 1950s–60s: Contract workers — musicians, artists, barbers across Asia, loggers in Kalimantan.
  • 1960s onward: Hospitality workers and Filipino bands at major hotels (see The First Filipinos in Bali).
  • 1970s–80s: As Bali's tourism boomed, kababayans diversified into spa, dive, salon, and culinary roles.
  • 1993: A Filipina named Maribeth Pascua released a song called "Denpasar Moon" that put a Bali city's name in every Indonesian's ear (see Denpasar Moon).
  • 1993: A Filipina chemist named Grace Tarjoto settled in Tabanan and would go on to help save Jatiluwih's heirloom red rice (see Grace M. Tarjoto).
  • 1952: A Manila-born painter named Antonio Blanco arrived in Bali and built what became one of Ubud's most-visited art museums (see Don Antonio Blanco).

2019 — 70th anniversary & the Filipinos in Bali

In 2019, on the 70th anniversary of diplomatic relations, the Philippine Embassy in Jakarta publicly recognised the Filipinos in Bali (BaliFILs) at a friendship celebration. It was a formal acknowledgement that BaliFILs was a fixture of Filipino life on the island.

2024 — 75 years

In 2024, the Philippines and Indonesia marked 75 years of unbroken friendship. Through wars, economic crises, ASEAN expansions, and political shifts on both sides, the relationship had not soured — a rare thing in modern diplomacy. Both countries reaffirmed their commitment to deepen ties in trade, defence, education, and people-to-people connection.

2025 — healthcare MOU

In May 2025, the two countries signed a memorandum of understanding on healthcare cooperation — opening pathways for joint training, professional mobility, and shared research. For Filipino health workers in Indonesia, and for Indonesian patients increasingly receiving care abroad, this is a meaningful step.

The trade picture

Indonesia is the Philippines' sixth-largest trading partner and a major source of imports. Coffee, palm oil, automotive components, coal, and processed foods flow in both directions. Tourism flows too — Filipinos are a steady stream of visitors to Bali, and Indonesians are an increasing share of visitors to the Philippines.

What this means for kababayans in Bali

Every Filipino in Bali is a small thread in this 75-year fabric. When you pay your banjar fee, sing OPM at a videoke night, hire a tukang to fix your scooter, attend Sunday Mass, or write a story for BaliFILs website — you are practising people-to-people diplomacy in the quiet, daily way that actually keeps two countries close. The treaties matter. The kababayans matter more.

Surviving Nyepi — The Day Bali Goes Silent

Filipinos in Bali•Published May 26, 2026

Once a year, the entire island of Bali stops. No flights take off. No cars move. Lights go out. Wi-Fi goes off in many areas. Even the airport closes. This is Nyepi, the Balinese Day of Silence — and if you live here, you need to plan for it. Here is how kababayans get through it well.

What Nyepi is

Nyepi is the Balinese Hindu New Year, marked by the lunar Saka calendar (usually falling in March). For 24 hours — typically from 6:00 AM on Nyepi day to 6:00 AM the next day — the entire island observes Catur Brata Penyepian, the "Four Restraints":

  • Amati Geni — no fire, no lights, no cooking with flame
  • Amati Karya — no work or activity
  • Amati Lelungan — no travel; stay indoors
  • Amati Lelanguan — no entertainment or pleasure

For Balinese, it is a day of meditation and inward reflection. For everyone else on the island, it is enforced quiet — and it is taken seriously.

The night before: Ogoh-Ogoh

The evening before Nyepi is the opposite of quiet. Villages parade enormous Ogoh-Ogoh — towering demon effigies built by youth groups over weeks — through the streets to symbolically drive out evil spirits. It is loud, theatrical, and one of the most spectacular cultural events you will see anywhere in Asia. Go watch. Bring water. Walk; you will not be able to drive.

What's enforced

  • Ngurah Rai International Airport closes for the full 24 hours. No arrivals, no departures.
  • Sea ports close. Fast boats to Lombok and Gili do not run.
  • Streets are patrolled by pecalang (traditional banjar guards). They will turn you back if you try to drive or walk around — politely but firmly.
  • Most TV channels and many mobile data services are shut off or restricted.
  • Lights must be off or curtains fully drawn after dark. Many hotels enforce blackout standards.
  • Restaurants, bars, shops — all closed.

What you're allowed to do

  • Stay inside your home or hotel. Move freely within.
  • Cook quietly using electricity (no open fires).
  • Read, rest, sleep, watch a downloaded movie, play board games.
  • If you are in a hotel, swimming pools may be open during the day but quiet.
  • Medical emergencies are exempt — ambulances run.

How to prep — your Nyepi checklist

  1. Three days before: note the exact date (check news/BaliFILs group) and decide where you'll spend the 24 hours.
  2. Two days before: book restaurant dinner for Nyepi eve if not cooking.
  3. The day before (morning):
    • Top up: groceries for two days, drinking water (lots), cooked food, snacks, fruit.
    • Top up: petrol, scooter battery, phone, power bank.
    • Withdraw cash (ATMs may not be reachable).
    • Download movies/podcasts/playlists offline.
    • Charge laptops, lamps, devices, kids' tablets.
    • Refill drinking water, gas canisters if used.
    • Buy candles (subtle warm light is permitted indoors with curtains closed).
  4. The day before (evening): watch the Ogoh-Ogoh parade, eat well, head home before midnight.
  5. Nyepi day: stay inside, keep noise down, enjoy the quietest day of your year.
  6. The day after: normal life resumes; airport reopens.

⚠️ If you have a flight on Nyepi

Re-book it. There are no flights into or out of Bali for the full 24-hour period. Travel insurance generally covers Nyepi as a "scheduled closure," not an emergency. Plan around it, not into it.

👨‍👩‍👧 With kids?

Pre-stock activities they can do without screens — books, puzzles, crafts, drawing supplies. The day is long for little ones. Bake bread. Play board games. Let them experience the deep quiet — most children find it magical once they're settled.

The hidden gift

For Filipinos used to constant noise — videoke, jeepneys, FB notifications — Nyepi is jarring at first and then, slowly, beautiful. You hear the wind. You hear the birds. You hear your own thinking. The Balinese have built into their calendar one full day a year when the whole island sits still and remembers that it exists. Receive it as the gift it is.

Visas & KITAS Explained for Filipinos in Bali

Filipinos in Bali • Published May 25, 2026 • Information current as of May 2026

Staying in Bali legally is the single most important thing you can get right. Immigration mistakes are stressful, expensive, and entirely avoidable. Here is a plain-language guide to how Indonesian visas work — and what it really takes to live and work on the island.

The golden rule: a tourist visa is not permission to work

This is the mistake that gets kababayans in trouble. A tourist or visit visa lets you holiday in Indonesia — it does not let you earn money here. Working on the wrong visa can lead to fines, detention, deportation, and a re-entry ban. If a job in Bali is the goal, you need a proper work permit. There are no real shortcuts.

Good news for Filipinos: visa-free short visits

As an ASEAN neighbor, Philippine passport holders can usually enter Indonesia visa-free for short tourist or social visits (commonly up to 30 days). This is great for an exploratory trip, a family visit, or attending a BaliFILs gathering — but remember, it is strictly for non-work purposes. To actually relocate and earn a living, you move on to a KITAS.

What is a KITAS?

KITAS stands for Kartu Izin Tinggal Terbatas — a Limited Stay Permit Card. It is the document that legally allows a foreigner to reside in Indonesia for an extended period, typically issued for terms of up to two years and renewable. There are several kinds of KITAS; the one most Filipinos in Bali need is the work KITAS.

How the work KITAS actually works

A work KITAS is built on top of an employer. You cannot simply buy one for yourself — an Indonesian company has to sponsor you. The process has three connected parts:

  • RPTKA — the employer's approved plan to hire a foreign worker for your specific role.
  • IMTA — the work permit itself, issued through the Ministry of Manpower, authorizing the company to employ you.
  • C312 work visa & KITAS — the visa you enter on, which is then converted into your KITAS stay-permit card after arrival.

Your sponsoring company must be properly registered in Indonesia — a foreign-owned company (PMA), a representative office, or a local company — and meet minimum capital requirements. A serious, legitimate employer will know this process well.

Who qualifies

To be approved for a work KITAS, you generally need to:

  • Hold a job title that matches a position foreigners are permitted to fill;
  • Earn a salary in line with national standards — broadly in the range of IDR 25–30 million per month;
  • Provide a clean criminal record and a valid health certificate;
  • Have qualifications or experience relevant to the role.

One reassuring note for kababayans: the Philippines is not on Indonesia's restricted-nationality list, so Filipino applicants are eligible for work permits like most other nationalities.

Timeline and cost

The full process — from the employer's RPTKA submission to your KITAS card in hand — typically takes 8 to 12 weeks. Costs are usually shouldered or coordinated by the employer, but a major one to be aware of is the DKP-TKA (formerly DPKK) — a foreign-worker compensation fee that funds local skills development. It is commonly billed at around US$100 per month and is paid upfront for the full duration of the permit.

Other KITAS types worth knowing

  • Family / dependent KITAS — for the spouse and children of a KITAS holder, so your family can stay with you.
  • Retirement KITAS — for older applicants who meet income or savings requirements and are not working.
  • Investor KITAS — for those who hold shares in an Indonesian PMA company.
  • Remote-worker visa (E33G) — for people employed by a company outside Indonesia who work online; useful for freelancers and digital professionals.

After holding a KITAS for several consecutive years — or through marriage to an Indonesian citizen — you may eventually qualify for a KITAP, a permanent stay permit.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Working on a tourist or visit visa "just for now."
  • Letting a tourist visa overstay — overstay fines accrue per day.
  • Using an unlicensed "fixer." Always use a reputable, licensed visa agent.
  • Not keeping copies of your passport, KITAS, and IMTA — carry digital and physical copies.

⚠️ Please verify before you act

Indonesian immigration rules, fees, and visa categories change frequently. This guide is for general orientation only — it is not legal advice. Always confirm the current requirements with a licensed Indonesian visa agent, your employer, or the official Directorate General of Immigration before making decisions or payments.

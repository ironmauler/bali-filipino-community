Denpasar Moon — The Filipina Whose Voice Became Bali's

Filipinos in Bali • Published May 26, 2026 • 8 min read

In the autumn of 1993, a song called Denpasar Moon began pouring out of every radio in Indonesia. It sat at the top of the charts for months, spawned dangdut and koplo cover versions, inspired a primetime TV drama, and became — almost by accident — the unofficial musical postcard of Bali for a whole generation. The voice carrying that song belonged to a Filipina from Bayombong, Nueva Vizcaya, named Maribeth Pascua. This is her story, and our song.

The voice from Bayombong

Maribeth Pascua was born on January 14, 1972, in Bayombong, the small capital of Nueva Vizcaya in northern Luzon. She came of age in the late 1980s with the kind of pure, controlled vocal that Filipinos seem to manufacture by the dozen — clear, expressive, equally at home with English ballads and OPM. In 1991, at age nineteen, she won the prestigious Voice of Asia competition, the regional song contest held in Alma-Ata, Kazakhstan. The win put her on Asia's radar. She had no idea it was about to put her at the centre of Indonesia's pop consciousness.

The unlikely origin of "Denpasar Moon"

The song itself didn't start in Manila or Jakarta or even Denpasar. It was written by a British progressive-rock bassist named Colin Bass, who had recorded under the stage name Sabah Habas Mustapha with the band 3 Mustaphas 3. Bass had a deep love of Southeast Asian music, and in 1992 he recorded the original version of "Denpasar Moon" in Jakarta. It was a gentle, breezy English-language ballad that name-checked the capital of Bali — a place Bass had visited and fallen for.

The original didn't break out commercially. Then Sony Music Japan bought the rights to the song and looked for a singer who could give it the right pop polish for an Asian audience. They handed it to Maribeth.

1993: the explosion

What happened next is one of the most extraordinary chapters of Filipino music in Asia. Maribeth's version of "Denpasar Moon" was released in 1993 and tore through Indonesia. Within months it was playing in taxis, malls, weddings, and karaoke lounges from Sabang to Merauke. It crossed genre lines effortlessly — soon there were dangdut and koplo versions, an Indonesian-language cover by the group Trio Laris, and a small army of imitators.

The song lodged itself in Indonesia's collective memory in the way only a few songs ever do — the way "Hotel California" or "Anak" did elsewhere. Decades later, ask any Indonesian over the age of forty to hum the chorus, and they probably can.

🌙 A funny twist

When Maribeth was first handed the song, she didn't actually know where Denpasar was. She only learned that it was the capital of Bali after promoting the song in Indonesia — and, by her own account, she fell in love with Bali and the country afterwards, not before.

Beyond the song: an Indonesian life

"Denpasar Moon" turned Maribeth into a household name in Indonesia. She followed it in 1994 with Born to Sing, and in 1995 starred as the lead in an Indonesian television series of the same name — Bulan di Atas Denpasar — on Indosiar. She toured relentlessly across the archipelago, becoming for many Indonesians the friendliest, most familiar face of Filipino pop in the 1990s.

Maribeth chose to make Indonesia her home for nearly two decades. She applied for Indonesian citizenship and lived for many years between performances, family, and quiet life in the country whose audience had taken her in so completely. Her citizenship application ultimately did not succeed, and after about 18 years, she returned to the Philippines. The departure was reported in the Indonesian press with real affection — a kababayan letting go of a country she had loved on stage and off.

Why this song belongs to us

For Filipinos in Bali — and for Filipinos in Indonesia more broadly — "Denpasar Moon" is more than a 90s pop hit. It is a quiet, persistent reminder that we have been here, in this country's living rooms and lounges and taxi dashboards, for a long time. A Filipina voice taught a generation of Indonesians how to sing the name of Bali's capital. That is not a small thing.

It also embodies something true about the Filipino musical presence across Asia. We've written about how Filipino bands became the soundtrack of five-star hotels across the continent (see The First Filipinos in Bali). Maribeth's story is the brightest, most public version of that same pattern: a Filipina trained in OPM, given a song written by a Brit, polished by a Japanese label, and embraced by Indonesia. Globalisation set to a karaoke chord.

The legacy

Three decades on, "Denpasar Moon" still surfaces on Indonesian nostalgia playlists, in covers by new generations of singers, in dangdut versions that play at wedding receptions in Java, and in karaoke rooms across Bali. Every time it plays, a Filipina from Bayombong is — quietly, lovingly — singing Bali back to itself.

For kababayans living in Bali today, that's a small piece of cultural ownership we get to carry. When someone here recognises that you're Filipino, and a smile flashes, and they hum a familiar tune — chances are good it's that one.

🎧 Listen

The song is easy to find on YouTube and most music streaming services — search for "Maribeth — Denpasar Moon". If you've never heard it, do yourself a favour. If you grew up hearing it, you already know the chorus.

Bali Cultural Brief: A Newcomer's Guide for Filipinos

Filipinos in Bali • Published May 26, 2026 • 12 min read

Bali is one of the warmest, most welcoming places on earth — but it is also one of the most culturally distinct. The Balinese have their own religion, calendar, language, and rhythm of life, layered on top of Indonesia's national culture. As Filipinos, we share some instincts with Indonesians, but Bali also asks us to unlearn a few habits. This brief is meant as a one-stop orientation: what to expect, how to be a good guest, and what to watch out for so your first months go smoothly.

The Bali you're stepping into

A Hindu island in a Muslim country

Indonesia is the largest Muslim-majority country in the world — but Bali is the major exception. Roughly 87% of Balinese practice Balinese Hinduism (Agama Hindu Dharma), a uniquely local form that blends Hindu traditions with Buddhism, ancestor veneration, and indigenous animism. It is not the same as Indian Hinduism. You will notice this every single day — in the temples on every corner, the offerings on every doorstep, and the ceremonies that pause normal life on a regular schedule.

Tri Hita Karana — the philosophy underneath everything

If you only remember one Balinese concept, make it this one: Tri Hita Karana — "the three causes of well-being." It describes harmony with God (parahyangan), harmony with people (pawongan), and harmony with nature (palemahan). It is the lens behind the offerings, the village layouts, the rice-field irrigation systems, and the social rituals you'll see all around you.

Offerings (canang sari)

Those little woven palm-leaf trays of rice, flowers, and incense you'll see everywhere — on doorsteps, dashboards, sidewalks, shop counters, even gas pumps — are canang sari. They're placed three times a day as a gesture of gratitude. They are sacred. Never, ever step on one or kick one aside. Always walk around them.

The ceremonial calendar

Balinese life runs on a separate calendar (the 210-day Pawukon plus the lunar Saka calendar). Big days you should know about:

  • Galungan & Kuningan — every 210 days, marking the victory of dharma over adharma and the return of ancestors. Tall bamboo poles (penjor) curve over the streets. Roads are quiet; many businesses close.
  • Nyepi (Day of Silence) — the Balinese New Year, usually March. For 24 hours the entire island shuts down: no flights, no driving, no leaving the house, no lights or sound, no work. Even the airport closes. The night before is the spectacular Ogoh-ogoh parade of demon effigies. Plan for Nyepi every year — stock up on food, water, and entertainment the day before.
  • Saraswati & Pagerwesi — days honouring knowledge and spiritual protection.
  • Odalan — every village temple has its own anniversary celebration. There are thousands of these across Bali every year.
  • Ngaben — elaborate cremation ceremonies. If you see a procession, pull over and let it pass with respect.

The banjar — your local community

Every Balinese village is organized into banjars — neighbourhood councils that run ceremonies, security, and community life. When you rent long-term, you'll usually pay a small monthly banjar fee. It's not a scam — it's how you become part of the neighbourhood. Introduce yourself to your kelian banjar (head) if your landlord suggests it. Good standing with your banjar makes life much smoother.

Names & titles

Balinese names follow birth order, regardless of gender:

  • 1st child — Wayan, Putu, or Gede
  • 2nd child — Made or Kadek
  • 3rd child — Nyoman or Komang
  • 4th child — Ketut
  • The 5th child cycles back to Wayan, and so on.

This is why you'll meet so many Wayans and Mades — perfectly normal. Caste-related prefixes you may also hear include Ida Bagus / Ida Ayu (Brahmin), Anak Agung or Cokorda, Dewa, and Gusti.

For polite address in Indonesian:

  • Pak — "Sir / Mr." (for any adult man)
  • Bu (Ibu) — "Ma'am / Mrs." (for any adult woman)
  • Mas — for a young man (also used for waiters, drivers)
  • Mbak — for a young woman

Language

You'll encounter three languages:

  • Bahasa Indonesia — the national language. Learn this. Even a hundred words will transform your daily life and earn instant goodwill.
  • Basa Bali — the local Balinese language, used within Balinese families and ceremonies. Most Balinese also speak Bahasa Indonesia.
  • English — widely spoken in tourist areas (Kuta, Seminyak, Canggu, Ubud, Sanur) but don't assume it works everywhere.

Daily life: what to expect

  • Driving is on the LEFT. This is the single biggest adjustment for Filipinos. You will instinctively look the wrong way crossing the street for weeks. Be very deliberate.
  • Scooters dominate the roads. A helmet is required by law. So is a valid licence — either an International Driving Permit (IDP) or an Indonesian SIM.
  • Sound is part of life. Gamelan music, roosters, motorbike calls to prayer in mixed neighbourhoods, and ceremony processions are normal — don't complain to your landlord about temple noise.
  • Animals. Bali has many free-roaming dogs (rabies is present — see "Watch outs" below). Monkeys near temples (Ubud, Uluwatu) will steal sunglasses, food, and phones. Do not feed them.
  • Two seasons. Dry (April–October) and rainy (November–March). Flash flooding and dengue are real during the rainy season.
  • Cash is still king in many warungs and small shops. Carry IDR.
  • Power. 220V, plug types C/F (round two-pin). Bring a small adapter for Philippine flat-pin chargers, or buy one cheaply in Bali.

Cultural Do's

  • Smile. A smile and a quiet greeting (Selamat pagi/siang/sore/malam) opens almost every door.
  • Use your right hand for giving, receiving, eating, and pointing. The left hand is traditionally considered unclean.
  • Point with your thumb, not your index finger — or gesture with an open hand.
  • Wear a sarong and sash (selendang) when entering a temple. Many temples lend or rent them.
  • Cover shoulders and knees at temples and during ceremonies.
  • Walk around offerings — never step over or on canang sari, even by accident.
  • Remove your shoes when entering homes, many shops, and any sacred space.
  • Lower your body slightly when passing in front of someone seated, especially elders or priests.
  • Ask permission before photographing people, ceremonies, or inside temples.
  • Stop for ceremony processions. Pull over, wait quietly, let them pass.
  • Use Pak, Bu, Mas, or Mbak when addressing people. It costs nothing and is appreciated.
  • Bargain politely at traditional markets — with a smile, never with anger.
  • Carry a copy of your KITAS and passport. Always.
  • Drive defensively, wear a helmet, use headlights day and night (required by Indonesian law for motorcycles).
  • Tip thoughtfully for good service — not required, but appreciated. Many restaurants already add a 5–10% service charge.

Cultural Don'ts

  • Don't touch anyone's head — including children. The head is sacred in Balinese belief.
  • Don't point your feet at people, shrines, or offerings; don't put your feet up on chairs or tables.
  • Don't step on or over offerings. If one is in your path, walk around.
  • Don't enter a temple without proper dress (sarong, sash, shoulders covered).
  • Don't enter temples or sacred areas when menstruating — this is the traditional rule, and signs at temples ask women to abstain.
  • Don't raise your voice or show public anger. Yelling at staff is deeply offensive and rarely fixes anything. Stay calm; it works better.
  • Don't engage in big public displays of affection. Holding hands is fine; long kisses and groping are not.
  • Don't sunbathe topless or nude at most beaches — it is illegal and offensive.
  • Don't sit higher than a priest during ceremonies or visits to a holy site.
  • Don't joke about religion, the Indonesian president, or politics — at best you cause offence, at worst you violate Indonesia's blasphemy or defamation laws.
  • Don't feed the monkeys at temples and forests; don't pet free-roaming dogs.
  • Don't haggle in fixed-price stores, malls, supermarkets, or restaurants. Markets and souvenir stalls are fine.
  • Don't take selfies in front of an active ceremony. Step back and observe respectfully.
  • Don't take "free" tours or follow strangers who offer to show you somewhere — most end at a hard-sell shop.

Things to watch out for

Drug laws — the most important warning

⚠️ Take this seriously

Indonesia has among the harshest drug laws in the world, including the death penalty for trafficking and many years in prison for possession — and this includes cannabis. A "friendly stranger" offering you something at a club may be a setup. Do not accept anything from strangers. If you're offered drugs, walk away. No exceptions, no curiosity, no "just this once." Kababayans have been jailed for years for tiny quantities.

Scooter safety

Scooter accidents are the number-one injury cause for newcomers. Drive at moderate speed, never without a helmet, never under the influence, and never with a phone in your hand. Get proper travel or expat health insurance before you ride. Wear long sleeves and closed shoes — road rash from a 30 km/h fall in a tank top is brutal.

Beaches & riptides

The southern surf beaches — Kuta, Legian, Seminyak, Canggu, Echo Beach — have strong riptides and undertows. Swim only between the red-and-yellow flags, never alone, and never after drinking. If caught in a rip, swim parallel to the shore, not against it. Many Filipinos who are confident swimmers underestimate Bali's surf.

Bali belly & food/water safety

Drink only bottled or filtered water — including for brushing your teeth in your first weeks. Skip ice at small warungs if you're new. Eat at busy warungs (fast turnover = fresh food). Stock oral rehydration salts at home; mild Bali belly is a rite of passage.

Rabies & animal bites

Rabies is endemic in Bali. If a dog, cat, or monkey bites or scratches you and breaks skin — even if it seems fine — go to a hospital the same day for post-exposure rabies vaccination. Don't wait. It is fatal once symptoms appear.

Earthquakes, tsunamis & volcanoes

Bali sits in a seismically active zone. Earthquakes happen; tsunamis are a possibility on the south coast. Mount Agung erupted as recently as 2017–2019. Learn your nearest evacuation route, especially if you live near the coast. The Magma Indonesia app gives official volcano updates.

Common scams

  • Rental deposit games — get a clear written contract (Indonesian and English), do a photo walk-through on day one, and never pay more than you must up front.
  • Taxi metering tricks — use Grab or Gojek apps. If you must use a street taxi, Bluebird is the most reputable; insist on the meter.
  • Currency exchange scams — only change money at banks or large reputable exchanges (PT Central Kuta, BMC, Dirgahayu Valuta). Tiny "no-commission" booths often shortchange you with sleight of hand.
  • Real-estate dreams — foreigners cannot hold freehold land in Indonesia. Be very skeptical of anyone selling you "ownership." Get an Indonesian lawyer before any property deal.
  • "Tourist police" stopping you for a fake infraction. Be polite, ask to go to a real police station, and don't pay roadside "fines."
  • Recruitment scams — a real job offer never asks you to pay a large placement fee. See our KITAS guide for the legitimate hiring path.

Visa & document discipline

Overstays accrue daily fines and can lead to deportation and re-entry bans. Renew your KITAS well before expiry. Always carry your KITAS card. See our Visas & KITAS guide and Filipino's KITAS how-to.

Specifically for Filipinos

  • Drive on the left. Said again because it matters. Most accidents involving Filipino newcomers happen in the first two months.
  • Dial down the volume. Pinoy banter is loud and direct; Balinese communication is softer and more indirect. A calm tone goes much further here.
  • Smile, then ask. "Maaf, Pak…" or "Permisi, Bu…" before a question. Don't barge into a request.
  • Religious life looks different. Bali has Catholic churches, Filipino Catholic fellowships, and Christian congregations, but Sunday Mass is smaller-scale than in the Philippines. See Faith, Fellowship & Fiesta.
  • Halal vs non-halal awareness. Bali itself is mostly Hindu, so pork (babi guling, in particular) is everywhere — great for many Filipinos. But Indonesia overall is Muslim, and many of your Indonesian colleagues will not eat pork. Be considerate when ordering for groups.
  • Don't assume Tagalog or English will get you through. Learn basic Bahasa Indonesia. Locals respond warmly to any effort.
  • Karaoke / videoke exists everywhere — but keep volume reasonable, especially in residential areas.
  • Group photos with BaliFILs flag are great. Filipinos waving Philippine flags during Balinese religious processions are not. Read the room.

🙏 The mindset that works

Think of yourself as a long-term guest in a sacred household. Be quiet, kind, curious, and respectful — and Bali will love you back. Most cultural mistakes are forgiven instantly when made with humility. The mistakes that aren't forgiven are the ones made loudly.

Rabies Is Still a Serious Threat in Bali — 34,561 Bites in Six Months

Filipinos in Bali•Published July 4, 2026

Bali's provincial health office has published the mid-year numbers, and they are sobering. Between January and June 2026, the island recorded 34,561 animal bite cases — an average of 205 bites every day — and five confirmed rabies deaths. If you live in Bali, or have kids or pets here, this is a moment to pay attention. Rabies is essentially 100% fatal once symptoms appear, but it is completely preventable when a bite is handled correctly on day one.

⚠️ If you or someone with you was bitten or scratched

Wash the wound with soap and running water for at least 15 minutes, then go to the nearest hospital or puskesmas today for post-exposure vaccination. Do not wait to "see if it gets worse." See the step-by-step below.

The numbers at a glance

  • 34,561 animal bite cases in Bali, January–June 2026
  • ~205 bites per day, on average
  • 26,106 victims received anti-rabies vaccine (VAR) through public health services
  • 5 rabies deaths in Bali so far in 2026
  • One recent Buleleng incident: three children bitten by a single suspected-rabid stray dog within one hour in Banyuasri

Where the bites are happening

The provincial breakdown shows bite cases spread across every regency — not just the tourist south. If you live outside Denpasar and Badung, the risk is still very real.

Why this matters for kababayans

Most Filipinos in Bali live in Badung (Canggu, Kuta, Jimbaran), Denpasar, Gianyar (Ubud), or Buleleng (the north) — five of the top six regencies by bite count. Many of us also walk to warungs, ride scooters through neighbourhoods with free-roaming dogs, and have young children in yards where strays occasionally wander through. That's the exposure profile the numbers describe.

Filipinos who grew up around friendly aspin dogs sometimes underestimate the risk here. In Bali, an unfamiliar dog — even one that looks calm — is not a dog to approach. Local dogs are usually territorial, often unvaccinated, and rabies is endemic on this island.

What the government is doing

Dr. I Gusti Ayu Raka Susanti of the Bali Health Office confirmed that "every bite case is managed by the local community health centre, including wound treatment and administration of anti-rabies vaccine." Vaccination and wound care at government puskesmas are available regardless of nationality and are the frontline of the response. Culling of suspected-rabid strays and mass canine vaccination drives continue in affected banjars.

But the ratio tells a story: 34,561 bites, only 26,106 post-exposure vaccinations. Roughly 1 in 4 bite victims recorded in Bali's system this year did not complete post-exposure prophylaxis. That gap is where the deaths come from.

If you or your child is bitten — the exact steps

  1. Immediately wash the wound for 15 full minutes with soap and running water. This alone dramatically reduces the amount of virus that can reach the nervous system. Do not skip or shorten this.
  2. Apply antiseptic (povidone-iodine or 70% alcohol) after washing.
  3. Go to a hospital or puskesmas the same day. Any of the major Bali hospitals — Siloam, BIMC, Kasih Ibu, Sanglah/RSUP Prof. Ngoerah — handle post-exposure rabies vaccination. Puskesmas do too, and are free.
  4. Get the post-exposure vaccine series (VAR). For severe bites (face, hands, deep wounds, or a stray with unknown vaccination status), you may also need rabies immunoglobulin (RIG). Ask specifically — it is not always offered by default.
  5. Complete the whole series. Missing doses is dangerous. Put the follow-up dates in your phone the moment you leave the clinic.
  6. Report the animal to the local banjar or the Dinas Peternakan (Livestock Service) so it can be traced, observed, or removed if it's a stray.

How to lower your risk in the first place

  • Consider pre-exposure rabies vaccination — three doses given over 21–28 days, available at BIMC, Siloam, and travel clinics. It doesn't eliminate the need for post-exposure treatment, but it simplifies it (fewer doses, no RIG needed) and buys you critical time if you're bitten somewhere remote.
  • Do not approach unfamiliar dogs or cats, even puppies and kittens. Do not feed strays outside your own compound.
  • Teach children the rule early: don't touch dogs you don't know; if a dog approaches, stand still, hands to your sides, don't scream or run.
  • Vaccinate your own pets annually and keep the certificate. If your dog bites someone, it protects them and you.
  • If you ride a scooter, be extra alert on narrow gang roads where dogs can dart out — many bites happen to riders whose leg was reached.
Rabies is 100% fatal — and 100% preventable when a bite is treated the same day. The people who die in Bali each year are almost always people who didn't get to a clinic in time.

Where to get help

  • Any puskesmas (community health centre) — free post-exposure vaccine, open across the island.
  • BIMC Hospital (Kuta, Nusa Dua, Ubud) — English-speaking, familiar with expat patients, offers both pre- and post-exposure protocols.
  • Siloam Hospital (Denpasar, Kuta) — 24/7 emergency, VAR and RIG usually available.
  • Sanglah / RSUP Prof. Ngoerah (Denpasar) — Bali's main public referral hospital.
  • Philippine Embassy Jakarta — Assistance to Nationals: jakartape.dfa.gov.ph if you need consular help in a serious medical case.

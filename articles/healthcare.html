Healthcare in Bali: Hospitals, Clinics & Insurance

Filipinos in Bali • Published May 25, 2026 • Information current as of May 2026

Nobody plans to get sick abroad — but a scooter scrape, a bout of food poisoning, or a sudden fever can happen to anyone. Knowing where to go, and being properly insured before you need it, is one of the most important things a Filipino in Bali can sort out.

⚠️ The hard truth first

Foreigners have no access to Indonesia's public healthcare system. There is no free safety net. If you are not insured, you pay every rupiah out of pocket — and private hospital bills add up frighteningly fast.

What healthcare in Bali is actually like

The good news: Bali has modern private and international hospitals with English-speaking doctors and a solid standard of care, concentrated around Denpasar, Kuta, Sanur, and Ubud. The catch: quality drops off in smaller public clinics, diagnoses can be inconsistent, and specialist care outside the main towns is limited. For anything serious, you want a reputable private hospital.

Hospitals & clinics to know

Well-regarded hospitals used by the expat community include:

  • BIMC Hospital — Kuta and Nusa Dua; popular with international patients.
  • Siloam Hospitals — a large national network with a Bali presence.
  • BaliMed Hospital — Denpasar.
  • Kasih Ibu Hospitals — several locations across Bali.
  • Bali Royal Hospital (BROS) — Denpasar.
  • Bali International Hospital — a newer international-standard facility in the Sanur health zone.

For minor issues — coughs, mild infections, prescriptions — a neighborhood klinik or a pharmacy (apotek) is usually faster and cheaper. Pharmacies are widespread and many basic medicines are inexpensive.

Insurance: do not skip this

The single biggest healthcare mistake newcomers make is assuming travel insurance is enough, or carrying no cover at all. Choose based on how long you are staying:

Travel insurance (short stays)

Suitable for visits of a few weeks to a few months. It typically covers emergency care, outpatient treatment, hospitalization, and limited dental, with premiums often around US$30–150 per month. Dental and some benefits are usually capped.

International health insurance (living here)

If Bali is now home, get a proper expat health plan. Established providers with strong hospital networks in Indonesia include Cigna Global, Allianz Care, AXA, and Prudential Indonesia. These plans cost more but give you real protection — including, on many plans, medical evacuation, which matters for serious cases.

🏢 Ask your employer

If you work on a KITAS, ask exactly what medical coverage your employer provides. Indonesia's national insurance scheme, BPJS Kesehatan, is generally tied to citizenship or permanent residency, but enrolment for foreign workers can depend on your permit and employer — get the specifics in writing before you assume you are covered.

What care costs without insurance

To understand the risk: a straightforward case of food poisoning treated at a private hospital can run US$500–1,000, and an ICU stay can climb into the tens of thousands of dollars. One uninsured emergency can wipe out months — or years — of savings. Insurance is not an expense; it is protection for everything you are working for.

In an emergency

  • Indonesia's general emergency number is 112.
  • For serious cases, going directly to a private hospital's emergency department (UGD/IGD) is often faster than waiting for an ambulance.
  • Save the address and phone number of your nearest reputable hospital in your phone now — before you ever need it.
  • Keep your insurance card, policy number, and passport copy easily accessible.

Everyday health tips for Bali

  • Drink bottled or filtered water; avoid tap water.
  • "Bali belly" is common — keep oral rehydration salts at home.
  • Ride carefully. Scooter accidents are the most common cause of serious injury for newcomers.
  • Use mosquito repellent — dengue is present in Indonesia.
  • Keep routine medications stocked; specific brands are not always available.

ℹ️ A note on this guide

This article is general information, not medical advice, and hospital services and insurance products change over time. Confirm current details directly with the hospital or insurer, and consult a qualified medical professional for any health concern.

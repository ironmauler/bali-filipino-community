A Taste of Home: Filipino Food in Bali

Filipinos in Bali • Published May 25, 2026 • 5 min read

Ask any Filipino abroad what they miss most, and somewhere near the top of the list is the food. A plate of sinigang on a rainy day, the smell of garlic rice in the morning, pancit on a birthday — food is how we carry home with us. The good news: in Bali, a taste of home is closer than you think.

Bali and the Philippines: closer on the plate than you'd guess

Filipino and Indonesian cuisines are cousins. Both lean on rice, garlic, shallots, ginger, soy sauce, and vinegar; both love grilling, frying, and slow-braising. You'll spot familiar friends on Balinese menus — sate (our barbecue's relative), lumpia (yes, Indonesia has its own spring rolls), bakso (meatball soup), and plenty of fried fish and rice. For a Filipino cook, Bali's markets are not a foreign country — they're a slightly different branch of the same family.

Eating out: finding kababayan flavours

Bali's food scene is enormous, and Filipino eateries and home-kitchen sellers do exist — often run by kababayans serving the community. Because these come and go, the best, most current list is the one the community keeps itself. Ask in BaliFILs group where to find good adobo, lechon for a party, or someone who takes orders for kakanin. Chances are a kababayan nearby is already cooking it.

Cooking at home: where to find the ingredients

Many Filipino staples are easy to find in Bali because Indonesia uses them too:

  • Rice, garlic, shallots, ginger, chillies, soy sauce, vinegar — everywhere, and cheap at any pasar (market) or supermarket.
  • Kangkong — known here as kangkung; identical, abundant, inexpensive.
  • Malunggay — Indonesians call it daun kelor; the same moringa leaves for your tinola.
  • Calamansi — look for jeruk limau or jeruk kalamansi, a close stand-in for that citrusy kick.
  • Banana leaves, pandan, coconut, lemongrass — all widely available.
  • Fish sauce & shrimp paste — Indonesian and Thai fish sauce work well; terasi is the local shrimp paste, a relative of bagoong.

Harder to find are specific Filipino-brand products — banana ketchup, certain vinegars and soy brands, longganisa, tocino, dried fish, and ready-made mixes. Larger supermarkets and Asian specialty stores sometimes stock them, and several kababayans in Bali import and resell Filipino goods. Again, BaliFILs group is your best directory for who's selling what right now.

Smart substitutions for kababayan cooks

  • No banana ketchup? A little tomato ketchup with brown sugar gets you close for Filipino spaghetti.
  • For adobo, local soy sauce (kecap asin) plus cane or coconut vinegar works beautifully.
  • Craving longganisa? Many kababayans make their own — recipes get shared in the group all the time.
  • Pandan and fresh coconut are cheap here, so kakanin and other native sweets are very doable.

Food is how we gather

At BaliFILs events, the potluck table is the heart of it all. Everyone brings a dish, the table fills with pancit, lumpia, lechon kawali, and rice, and suddenly a venue in Sanur feels like a fiesta back home. Food is more than nourishment for Filipinos abroad — it is memory, identity, and the easiest way to say "you belong here."

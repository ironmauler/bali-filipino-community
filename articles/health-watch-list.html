A Health Watch-List for Filipinos in Bali

Filipinos in Bali•Published May 26, 2026

Bali is generally a healthy place to live — sunshine, fresh produce, slower pace, plenty of movement. But there are specific risks the island throws at newcomers that catch kababayans off-guard. This is the watch-list. Read it once, screenshot it, share it with your housemate. For the broader hospital and insurance guide, see Healthcare in Bali.

1. Bali belly & food/water safety

Almost every newcomer gets it — diarrhea, cramps, often a low fever. Usually a bacterial or viral GI upset from food/water contamination. Prevent it: drink only bottled or filtered water (including for tooth-brushing in your first weeks); skip ice at small warungs; eat at busy spots with fast turnover; wash hands before eating. Treat it: rest, hydrate with oral rehydration salts (sold at any apotek as oralit), eat plain rice and bananas. If blood appears, fever stays above 38.5°C, or symptoms last beyond 3 days, see a doctor.

2. Dengue fever

Mosquito-borne; risk year-round but worst in the rainy season (November–March). High fever, severe headache, joint pain ("breakbone" feeling), and sometimes a rash. Prevent: use mosquito repellent with DEET or picaridin, especially morning and late afternoon (the Aedes mosquito bites in daylight); install screens; clear standing water around your home. If you suspect dengue: get a blood test promptly. Manage with rest, fluids, and paracetamol — avoid ibuprofen and aspirin, which can worsen bleeding. Severe dengue needs hospital monitoring.

3. Rabies — take this seriously

Bali has had ongoing rabies outbreaks in dogs and cats. If any animal scratches or bites you and breaks skin — even slightly, go to a hospital the same day for post-exposure rabies vaccination. Don't wait, don't "see if it gets worse." Rabies is essentially 100% fatal once symptoms appear, but the post-exposure series is highly effective if started promptly. Pre-exposure rabies vaccination is a smart investment if you're staying long-term, especially with kids or pets.

4. Scooter accidents

The single biggest cause of serious injury for newcomers. Reasons: driving on the LEFT (new for Filipinos), unfamiliar traffic patterns, slick roads in the rain, riding at night, alcohol, no helmet. Prevent: wear a real helmet (not the rental's loose one); long sleeves and closed shoes; never under the influence; ride slow until you've done 1,000 km here; install a phone holder; use headlights day and night (required by law). Get proper insurance.

5. Sun & dehydration

Bali sits 8 degrees south of the equator. UV is intense. Sunburn and heat exhaustion are real risks even on cloudy days. Prevent: SPF 30+ daily, hat, sunglasses, electrolyte drinks (coconut water is your friend), avoid midday outdoor exertion.

6. Skin issues

Humidity + heat = fungal skin infections (athlete's foot, ringworm), heat rash, and a bacterial skin infection called impetigo on small cuts. Prevent: stay dry; change wet clothes promptly; treat small wounds with antiseptic immediately; don't ignore an itchy patch.

7. Respiratory: dust, mould, & air quality

Bali air is mostly clean — but seasonal volcanic ash, construction dust, and indoor mould (from humidity) can trigger asthma and allergies. Prevent: dehumidifier in bedrooms, clean A/C filters quarterly, mask up during heavy dust days.

8. Sexual health

Indonesia has a moderate burden of HIV, hepatitis B, and other STIs. Use condoms, get tested annually, and discuss PrEP with a doctor if relevant. Reputable hospitals offer confidential testing.

9. Mental health

Loneliness, homesickness, culture stress, and isolation are real. They can also creep up months in. Prevent: build community early (BaliFILs, church, sports, work friends), keep regular video calls home, get out of the house daily, exercise. If you're struggling, reach out — to a kababayan, to a counsellor (telehealth options work well from Bali), or to the Embassy's Assistance to Nationals if it's serious.

10. Volcanoes, earthquakes, tsunamis

Bali sits in an active seismic zone. Mount Agung last erupted 2017–2019. Tsunami risk exists on the south coast. Prevent: install the Magma Indonesia app for volcano alerts; learn your nearest tsunami evacuation route if you live coastal; pack a small go-bag (passport copy, KITAS, cash, charger, water, first-aid).

Vaccines worth having

  • RoutineTetanus, MMR, hepatitis A & B
  • Strongly recommendedTyphoid, Japanese encephalitis, pre-exposure rabies
  • SeasonalAnnual flu shot
  • For kidsComplete the routine schedule per your paediatrician

⚠️ Not medical advice

This is a general orientation, not a substitute for medical consultation. Talk to a qualified doctor about your specific health, especially for vaccines, chronic conditions, pregnancy, and medications. See Healthcare in Bali for hospital and insurance details.

Grace M. Tarjoto — The Filipina Who Helped Save Jatiluwih's Red Rice

Filipinos in Bali • Published May 26, 2026 • 9 min read

If you have ever eaten the deep, nutty, red-husked rice grown in the famous terraces of Jatiluwih, you have eaten a grain that a Filipina helped save. Her name was Grace M. Tarjoto. She was a chemical engineer from the Philippines who, together with her Indonesian husband Heru, spent the better part of two decades quietly making sure that one of Bali's oldest heirloom crops — and the farmers who grow it — had a future. This is her story.

From the Philippines to the rice terraces

Grace trained as a chemist at the University of San Agustin in the Philippines. According to Indonesian press reporting, she had been living in Tabanan regency, Bali, since 1993. She had first visited as an ordinary tourist; the rolling green amphitheatre of Jatiluwih Village, set high on the slopes of Gunung Batukaru, refused to let her leave. With her husband Heru Tarjoto — an environmental and tool-design engineer — she made the village her home.

Jatiluwih is no ordinary place. Its hand-cut rice terraces are part of the UNESCO World Heritage subak system, the cooperative irrigation tradition that has shaped Balinese culture for more than a thousand years. The signature crop is a striking heirloom variety known as beras merah Jatiluwih — Jatiluwih red rice — celebrated for its colour, its earthy flavour, and the difficulty of growing it well.

The problem she saw

Red rice grows slowly. Where ordinary white rice in Bali could be harvested two or even three times a year, Jatiluwih's red rice yielded only one harvest annually. Yet farmers were being paid the same per-kilo price as commodity white-rice growers. The economics quietly squeezed a generation of subak farmers, and with them an ancient way of working the land.

Grace and Heru saw it clearly, and decided to do something about it.

2003: a mill, a method, and a market

In 2003, the Tarjotos developed modern rice-processing facilities in Jatiluwih built around a custom mill Heru designed. The trick was to husk and clean the grain without stripping away the red-gold bran layer that gives the rice its name, its nutrition, and its market value. Grace — the chemist — handled the science, the standards, and the marketing.

The model was simple and revolutionary in its plainness: buy the red rice from farmers at a fair, higher price, mill it well, package it with care, and sell it to the audiences who would value it — first across Indonesia, then to organic and specialty markets abroad. Farmers, for the first time in a long while, were paid like the custodians of a heritage crop they actually were.

Founding the farmers' association

To make the model durable, Grace founded the Jatiluwih Organic Red Rice Association of Farmers. The cooperative gave growers a unified voice, a stable buyer, and a steady push toward organic methods. The point was never branding; the point was income, dignity, and continuity. Grace was widely quoted in Indonesian press as saying the heritage of red rice in Jatiluwih was a thousand years old — and that losing it would mean losing the history and the life of the village itself.

Recognition: Slow Food's Ark of Taste

Quietly, the work was noticed. Jatiluwih red rice was inducted into the Slow Food Foundation's Ark of Taste, the international catalogue of endangered heritage foods worth defending. For an heirloom grain from a small Balinese village to land on a global list curated out of Turin is no accident; it is the result of years of careful stewardship — and of someone making sure the world could find it.

Becoming Indonesian

Grace eventually obtained Indonesian citizenship (WNI). She did not just live in Bali; she chose Bali, and Bali chose her back. She was profiled by Indonesia's national press — Detik ran a photo feature titled "Grace, the Figure Behind Bali's Rice Fields"; ANTARA News Bali wrote about her work preserving the red-rice heritage; NusaBali kept a dedicated tag of stories about her over the years.

January 16, 2018

On Wednesday, January 16, 2018, Grace M. Tarjoto was found to have passed away in her bedroom at the family home in Banjar Gunungsari, Jatiluwih. She was 62 years old. According to Tribun-Bali, she had been suffering from flu-like illness for about a week beforehand and had stayed at home to recover. There were no indications of foul play; her family and the local authorities recorded her passing as the result of the illness she had been fighting.

News of her death moved quickly through Jatiluwih, where she was known by name in every farming household, and through the Filipino community across Bali. A pioneer had been lost. The terraces went on, as they have for centuries, but quieter for the loss of the woman who had argued the world into paying them their worth.

🌾 Her legacy, in every grain

The Jatiluwih Organic Red Rice Association of Farmers continues. Heru Tarjoto continued operations after Grace's passing. The brand Jatiluwih Organic Red Rice is now sold across Indonesian groceries and specialty stores, and exported. Every bag of the heirloom red rice — and every bowl of nutty, earthy goodness it produces — is, in some real sense, a tribute to her.

Why she belongs on this site

The Filipinos in Bali is, by definition, made of people who chose to make a life here. Grace M. Tarjoto chose harder than most. She did not run a hotel, sing a hit, or sit in a foreign embassy. She solved an unglamorous economic problem for a community of subak farmers in a village most of the world has never heard of — and in doing so, helped protect a thousand-year-old food tradition for the next generation. That is what a notable kababayan looks like.

If you live in Bali, you can honour her quietly the next time you shop: buy authentic Jatiluwih organic red rice, cook it slowly, share it with kababayans, and tell the story of who put it on your table.

Paalam, Grace. Salamat sa ginawa mo para sa Jatiluwih, sa Bali, at sa atin.

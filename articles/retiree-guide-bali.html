Bali for Filipino Retirees

Filipinos in Bali•Published May 26, 2026

For Filipinos thinking about retirement, Bali is one of the most considered options in Asia — closer to home than Europe, friendlier than Hong Kong, gentler than Bangkok, and with a Filipino community that already exists. Here is the honest version of what retiring here looks like.

The retirement KITAS

Indonesia offers a dedicated retirement KITAS (sometimes called the Lansia KITAS) for foreigners aged 55 and over. It is a renewable stay permit that does not allow you to work — it's for living, not earning. Typical requirements include:

  • Minimum age 55
  • Proof of pension or independent income (commonly cited at around US$1,500/month, though check current figures)
  • A rental or property lease in Indonesia (commonly minimum value thresholds apply)
  • Health and life insurance covering Indonesia
  • Employ at least one Indonesian household worker (a common requirement)
  • Clean criminal record and valid passport (18+ months validity)

The application is processed through a sponsoring agent in Indonesia. Most retirees use a licensed visa agent rather than DIY. Rules and thresholds change — always verify current requirements before committing.

A realistic monthly retirement budget

Sample budgets (single retiree, Sanur)

  • Modest~US$1,200–1,600 / month
  • Comfortable~US$1,800–2,500 / month
  • Couple, comfortable~US$2,500–3,800 / month
  • With private health insuranceadd US$200–500 / month

Sanur is the most popular retiree base — calm, walkable, healthcare nearby, established expat and kababayan community. Lovina (north Bali) and Ubud are quieter alternatives. Canggu and Seminyak are noisier and more expensive.

Healthcare in retirement

This is the biggest single planning item. Indonesia has no public healthcare for foreigners; private hospitals are good but expensive without insurance. Options:

  • International expat health insurance — Cigna Global, Allianz Care, AXA, Prudential Indonesia. Comprehensive, with medical evacuation. Expensive — premiums for retirees can be US$3,000–10,000+ per year depending on age and conditions.
  • Combination strategy — local hospital cash account + emergency-evacuation-only insurance. Cheaper, riskier.
  • Self-fund + travel back to PH — for major care, fly home to your PhilHealth/HMO network. Workable for predictable care; risky for emergencies.

See Healthcare in Bali and A Health Watch-List for Filipinos in Bali for more.

What life actually looks like

Days run slow. Morning walk by the beach. Coffee with neighbours. Sunday Mass. Mid-morning errands. Long lunch. Afternoon nap. Cocktail hour. Sunset. Friends, family video calls, a quiet dinner. Most retirees report the same surprise: the days are full, but never frantic. Bali rewards a slow life.

The trade-offs honest retirees mention

  • Distance from grandkids — 4–5 hours by air to Manila/Cebu. Plan for visits both ways.
  • Healthcare logistics — specialist care for complex conditions sometimes requires travel to Jakarta or Singapore.
  • Language — Bahasa Indonesia is much easier than Mandarin or Thai, but you'll want a basic 200 words.
  • Property limits — foreigners cannot own freehold land. Rent for the long term; don't believe "nominee" pitches.
  • Bureaucracy — KITAS renewals, insurance renewals, banking — there's always a form. A good agent is worth it.

How kababayan retirees thrive here

  • Join BaliFILs early. The community welcomes retirees warmly and gets you plugged into Sunday Mass, gatherings, and trusted referrals.
  • Pick a small home. Big villas are lovely, then exhausting. A simple 1–2 bedroom is easier to manage.
  • Keep moving. Walk, swim, yoga, gardening — the most successful retirees stay active daily.
  • Volunteer. Many kababayan retirees mentor newer arrivals, teach English, support churches, or help local farmers. Purpose extends life.
  • Plan your end-of-life basics. A will, a healthcare proxy, repatriation insurance, instructions for your family — calm planning now spares stress later.

🌅 The litmus test

Before you commit, come live in Bali for three months, then go home for a month, then come back for three more. If you still want to be here after that cycle, you've found your retirement. If you miss home too much, your retirement is somewhere else — and that's a perfectly good answer to find out.

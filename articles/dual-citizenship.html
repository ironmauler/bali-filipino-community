Dual Citizenship (RA 9225) for Filipinos in Indonesia

Filipinos in Bali•Published May 26, 2026

If you ever lost your Philippine citizenship because you became a citizen of another country, you can get it back. The law is called Republic Act 9225 — the Citizenship Retention and Re-acquisition Act of 2003. Here is what it does, who qualifies, and a major Indonesia-specific catch you need to understand.

The big idea

Before RA 9225, a Filipino who became a citizen of another country automatically lost their PH citizenship. RA 9225 fixed that. It allows:

  • Filipinos who later became foreign citizens to re-acquire their PH citizenship.
  • Children of those Filipinos (unmarried, below 18) to derive PH citizenship through their parents' re-acquisition.
  • The recovered status is "dual" — you legally hold both PH and your other citizenship.

Re-acquisition is done through a Philippine Embassy or Consulate — for kababayans in Indonesia, that's the Embassy in Jakarta.

⚠️ The big Indonesia catch

Indonesia does not recognise dual citizenship for adults. A Filipino who naturalises as an Indonesian (WNI) must, under Indonesian law, renounce their previous citizenship. In practice this means: you cannot legally be a Filipino and an Indonesian adult citizen at the same time under Indonesian law. RA 9225 lets the PH side recognise you again — but Indonesia's side may treat re-acquisition as inconsistent with your WNI status. Consult both an Indonesian immigration lawyer and the PH Embassy before re-acquiring if you've already become WNI.

Who RA 9225 actually helps in Indonesia

The most common cases that work cleanly:

  • Filipinos who became citizens of a third country (USA, Canada, Australia, etc.) and are now living/working in Indonesia on a KITAS. They re-acquire PH citizenship via the Embassy without an Indonesia conflict.
  • Children born abroad to a former Filipino parent who never registered them — they may qualify derivatively.
  • Filipinos planning ahead who want to preserve PH ties before considering Indonesian citizenship later.

The process at the Embassy

Documents you'll typically need

  • Original and photocopy of your PH birth certificate (PSA)
  • Original and photocopy of your foreign passport and certificate of naturalisation
  • Original and photocopy of your old PH passport, if available
  • Marriage certificate (PSA) if married and applying with spouse-related details
  • Birth certificates of unmarried children below 18 (PSA), if applying derivatively for them
  • Recent passport-sized photos (check current spec)
  • Completed petition form and applicable fees
  • Apostilled supporting docs where required (PH joined Apostille Convention in 2019)

The steps

  1. Book an appointment with the PH Embassy in Jakarta.
  2. Submit the petition with documents.
  3. If approved, take the Oath of Allegiance to the Republic of the Philippines.
  4. Receive your Identification Certificate (IC).
  5. You can now apply for a new Philippine passport as a dual citizen.

What dual citizenship gets you

  • The right to vote in PH national elections (after overseas voter registration).
  • The right to own land in the Philippines as a Filipino.
  • The right to work, study, and conduct business in PH as a citizen.
  • Visa-free entry to the Philippines (you re-enter as a citizen).
  • Eligibility for certain government services tied to citizenship.

Tax considerations

Dual citizenship can create tax-residency questions. The Philippines generally taxes Filipino citizens only on income earned in the Philippines (resident or non-resident); but rules around large repatriations, real-estate income, and capital gains can be complex. Consult a tax adviser if your situation involves significant assets.

If you're married to an Indonesian

Children of mixed Filipino-Indonesian marriages have a separate special pathway under Indonesian law (Law No. 12 of 2006 allows limited dual citizenship for minor children of mixed marriages until age 18, after which they must choose). Combined with RA 9225 on the PH side, this can preserve options for your children — but it is genuinely complex. Don't navigate it alone.

📞 Get qualified advice

RA 9225 is generally straightforward for Filipinos in third countries living in Indonesia. It is not straightforward for Filipinos who have already become WNI. The Embassy can guide you through the PH side; a licensed Indonesian immigration lawyer is essential for the WNI side. This article is general orientation — not legal advice.

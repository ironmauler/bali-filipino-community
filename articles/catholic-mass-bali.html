Where to Find Catholic Mass in Bali

Filipinos in Bali•Published May 26, 2026

For many Filipinos in Bali, Sunday Mass is more than a religious obligation — it is a piece of home you can step into for an hour each week. Bali is majority Hindu, but the island has an established Catholic community with parishes across Denpasar, the south, Ubud, and the resort areas. Here is how to find yours.

The Catholic landscape in Bali

Bali falls under the Roman Catholic Diocese of Denpasar, with parishes in Denpasar, Kuta, Tuban, Nusa Dua, Ubud, and other towns. Mass is typically celebrated in Bahasa Indonesia, with some parishes offering English-language Masses for international congregants. A few churches near tourist and expat areas also host Filipino prayer groups, choirs, and occasional Tagalog liturgical elements during special seasons.

Well-known parishes Filipinos attend

  • Gereja Katolik Roh Kudus (Holy Spirit Catholic Church) — Tuban / Kuta area. Convenient for kababayans working in the airport/Kuta/Seminyak corridor; has multiple Sunday Masses.
  • Gereja Katolik St. Yoseph (St. Joseph Catholic Church) — Denpasar (Kepundungan). One of the older Catholic parishes on the island; central and accessible.
  • Gereja Katolik St. Fransiskus Xaverius — Kuta. Well-attended international parish; English Mass typically offered.
  • Gereja Katolik Maria Bunda Segala Bangsa — Nusa Dua. Serves the resort corridor where many kababayans live and work.
  • Gereja Katolik St. Yohanes Pemandi — Ubud-area. For kababayans in the central highlands.

Schedules change. Always check the parish website, Facebook page, or call ahead the week before — especially around major feasts.

Big seasons for Filipino Catholics

  • Simbang Gabi — the nine dawn Masses leading to Christmas (December 16–24). Some Bali parishes accommodate Simbang Gabi requests from Filipino communities; coordinate with your parish priest in advance.
  • Holy Week & Easter — full liturgies are celebrated; expect packed churches on Easter Sunday.
  • Santo Niño (third Sunday of January) — Filipino devotional gatherings happen informally; ask in BaliFILs group.
  • Flores de Mayo & Santacruzan — community-run, not parish-run; organised by Filipino families if you have enough hands.

How to plug into Catholic life as a kababayan

  1. Pick a home parish near where you live or work; introduce yourself to the parish office.
  2. Join a ministry — choir, lectors, ushers, hospitality, catechism. Filipinos are warmly welcomed; many ministries are short-handed.
  3. Find a Filipino prayer group — small Bible-study circles meet in members' homes across the island. Ask in BaliFILs.
  4. Bring the family. Mass is one of the easiest ways for Filipino children to see other Filipino families in Bali.

🙏 Be respectful, both ways

Bali is majority Hindu and proud of it. Catholic life here is welcomed but modest. Dress conservatively for Mass (shoulders and knees covered), keep volume down, and respect the wider Balinese context — for example, do not display Catholic processions in ways that would intersect with active temple ceremonies. The two faiths live well as neighbours when each shows the other quiet respect.

Beyond Catholic Mass

Bali also has Protestant and Evangelical congregations, and Filipino Christian fellowships are active across denominations. If you are Christian but not Catholic, ask in BaliFILs group — kababayans will point you to the right community.

Parish schedules and contacts shift. If you spot something out of date — or want to nominate a parish to be added — please email balifilipinos@gmail.com. We'd love to maintain a verified Mass directory for the community.

The Banjar — How to Be a Good Neighbour in Bali

Filipinos in Bali•Published May 26, 2026

Move into a neighbourhood in Bali and you'll soon hear the word banjar. Pay attention — the banjar is one of the oldest, most quietly powerful institutions in Balinese life. Understanding it is the difference between being a tenant who lives somewhere and being a neighbour who belongs somewhere.

What a banjar actually is

A banjar is a Balinese neighbourhood council — the basic unit of social organisation in a village. Every Balinese village (desa) is divided into one or more banjars, and every adult resident is a member. The banjar organises ceremonies, mediates disputes, runs cremations, watches the streets, takes care of widows, and decides who hosts the next festival. It is older than the Indonesian state and, in many ways, closer to people's daily lives.

The kelian banjar

The elected (or appointed) head of the banjar is called the kelian banjar. He (it is usually a he) is your first point of contact for anything community-related — from getting a residence letter to asking permission for a noisy party. Most kelian banjars are patient, kind, and used to dealing with foreigners. Treat them with respect and they will go out of their way for you.

What the monthly fee pays for

If you rent long-term in a residential area, expect a small monthly banjar fee — often Rp 50,000–200,000 per household. This is not a scam. It pays for:

  • Security patrols (the pecalang, traditional neighbourhood guards)
  • Garbage collection in many areas
  • Ceremony preparation — the village's share of temple festivals and processions
  • Mutual aid for funerals, illnesses, weddings
  • Public spaces — the bale banjar meeting hall, decorations, road maintenance

Paying your banjar fee on time is one of the simplest, most effective ways to integrate.

How to introduce yourself

Within your first week or two, ask your landlord to bring you to the kelian banjar to introduce yourself. Bring:

  • A copy of your KITAS and passport
  • A copy of your rental contract
  • A small, friendly gift is appreciated but not required (a box of biscuits, coffee, fruit)

Be warm. Smile. Speak any Bahasa you have. Mention you are Filipino — the banjar may already know other kababayans nearby.

The unwritten rules of being a good neighbour

  • Show up for ceremonies. When the street is closed for a temple festival, don't complain — go look. Take off your shoes if you enter the temple yard, dress modestly, smile, watch.
  • Quiet hours. Loud music after 10 PM in residential areas is rude and gets reported. Videoke before 9 PM is generally fine.
  • Help when asked. If the banjar is fundraising for a member's funeral, contribute. It's small money for you and means a great deal.
  • Don't park across processions. Move your scooter, pull over, wait. Always.
  • Respect offerings. Walk around canang sari placed on the street, never over.
  • Mind your dogs. If you have one, keep it from troubling neighbours or temple offerings.
  • Greet, greet, greet. A "Om Swastiastu" or "Selamat pagi, Pak/Bu" goes far.

📜 If you live in a Filipino-style closed compound

Many kababayans in Bali live in expat-style gated complexes that fall outside the traditional banjar system. You may not pay a banjar fee. But the surrounding village still exists; the same rules of respect apply when you step outside your gate. Don't assume the gate makes you exempt from local life.

When things go wrong

If you have a problem with a neighbour, with a noise issue, or with petty theft — bring it to the kelian banjar first, before the police. Most issues resolve faster and more fairly through the banjar than through any other channel. Stay calm, speak softly, and accept that the resolution may not look like what you expected. That is Bali working as Bali works.

🌺 The deeper point

The banjar is not just bureaucracy — it is the living expression of Tri Hita Karana, harmony with God, people, and nature. When you contribute to it, you are participating in a thousand-year-old social compact. Kababayans who understand this are the ones villages remember fondly long after they've moved away.

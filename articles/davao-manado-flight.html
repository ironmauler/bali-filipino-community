A Direct Davao–Manado Flight Is Back on the Table — Why It Matters to BaliFils

Filipinos in Bali•Published June 4, 2026

For most of us moving between the Philippines and Indonesia, the trip means a long day of connecting flights and layovers. So this is worth watching: officials in Davao City are working to bring back a direct flight to Manado, in Indonesia's North Sulawesi — a route that could turn a journey of roughly 19 hours into a flight of under two hours.

The numbers

Davao City and Manado sit only about 625 km apart. Yet with no direct service today, getting between them takes around 19 hours via connecting flights. A non-stop flight would take roughly 1 hour and 55 minutes — barely longer than a Manila–Davao domestic hop.

Who is behind it

The push is being led by the Mindanao Development Authority (MinDA), the Department of Tourism (DOT), and the Department of Trade and Industry, working under the BIMP-EAGA framework — the East ASEAN Growth Area that links Brunei, Indonesia, Malaysia, and the Philippines to grow trade, investment, and tourism across the region.

Why earlier attempts didn't last

This isn't the first try. The Davao–Manado route was first flown in 2007 by Sriwijaya Air, with Wings Air following the next year. Both stopped because of low passenger and cargo numbers. A 2014 revival as a chartered service ran into the same problem, and the pandemic later wiped out what momentum remained.

Romeo Montenegro, MinDA's Deputy Executive Director, has stressed that simply restarting flights isn't enough — the route only survives if there's steady demand behind it from tourism, trade, and investment.

This time, the approach is "demand-first" — build the reasons to travel before the planes take off.

So agencies are now developing tourism circuits, trade exchanges, and local-government partnerships before asking an airline to commit. It's a slower start, but a more durable one.

What's happening now

During a trade mission to North Sulawesi in early April 2026, a Mindanao delegation met with executives from Indonesian carrier TransNusa, who signalled genuine private-sector interest in serving the route. The airline indicated it is ready to resume operations, pending a market-viability study and the usual regulatory approvals. MinDA is set to coordinate with the Civil Aviation Authority of the Philippines and the Civil Aeronautics Board on the BIMP-EAGA support policies that would make the route work.

DOT Region XI Director Tanya Rabat-Tan called reviving the route a priority, pointing to opportunities in education, medical and wellness tourism, diving, and adventure travel. One idea already on the table: a dive circuit linking Davao with Manado's famous Bunaken National Park. The mission also advanced the Davao Triangle Gateway Corridor, a plan to position eastern Mindanao as a single gateway into the wider BIMP-EAGA region.

Why this matters for kababayans

  • A real shortcut. A direct Mindanao–Indonesia hop would make trips between our two countries far easier — especially for members with family or business on both sides.
  • More than tourism. The plan leans on education, medical, and wellness travel — the kinds of practical, repeat trips many of us already make.
  • Demand is the deciding factor. Officials say the route lives or dies on whether enough people actually use it. A community that regularly moves between the Philippines and Indonesia is exactly the steady demand they're counting on.

The bigger picture

Officials frame the effort as part of President Ferdinand Marcos Jr.'s priority of making Mindanao a strategic hub for ASEAN trade and tourism. For now, no flights are booked and no dates are set — this is still in the planning and demand-building stage. But the combination of government coordination and a willing airline makes this the most serious attempt in years.

We'll keep the community posted as this develops. If a direct Davao–Manado route does take off, it would be a meaningful step toward easier movement between the Philippines and Indonesia — something this group has always cared about.

This article summarises reporting from Philippine news outlets and the Mindanao Development Authority as of June 2026. Routes, carriers, and timelines may change as plans develop.

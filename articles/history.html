The Story of Filipinos in Bali

Filipinos in Bali • Published May 25, 2026 • 7 min read

For most travelers, Bali is a postcard. For thousands of Filipinos, it is something else entirely — a workplace, a home, and increasingly, a community. The story of how kababayans came to the Island of the Gods is part of a much older story of Filipinos building lives across Southeast Asia.

A friendship older than most of us

The Philippines and Indonesia are close neighbors — separated by a narrow stretch of sea and joined by centuries of shared culture, trade, and Austronesian roots. The two countries formally established diplomatic relations on November 24, 1949, and signed a Treaty of Friendship in Jakarta on June 21, 1951.

That relationship has been remarkably steady ever since. In 2024, the two nations marked 75 years of diplomatic ties, and the friendship continues to deepen through trade, education, and people-to-people connections. Every Filipino who lives in Bali today is, in a small way, part of that long story of two neighbors who chose to stay close.

The first kababayans in Indonesia

Filipino migration to Indonesia is not new. As early as the 1950s and 1960s, Filipino contract workers began moving across Asia. Musicians, artists, and barbers found work in East Asia, while Filipino loggers worked in Kalimantan, the Indonesian side of Borneo. Filipino entertainers, in particular, became a familiar presence in hotels and lounges throughout the region.

Over the decades, the profile of Filipinos in Indonesia shifted. Today, many are skilled professionals — teachers in international schools, advertising specialists, and accountants in the finance sector. According to Philippine government figures, roughly 7,400 Filipinos were living in Indonesia as of 2022, most of them based in Jakarta, with established communities in Surabaya and other cities.

Why Bali?

Bali's rise as one of the world's premier destinations changed everything. As tourism boomed, the island's hotels, resorts, spas, dive centers, restaurants, and entertainment venues needed exactly what Filipino workers are widely known for: fluent English, warm and attentive service, adaptability, and musical talent.

Filipino bands had long been a fixture of hotel lounges across Asia, and Bali was no exception. Alongside the musicians came spa and wellness therapists, dive instructors, hairdressers and beauticians, chefs and cooks, and hospitality staff of every kind. Bali offered something many other postings could not — a tropical setting, a lower cost of living than the big Asian capitals, and a relaxed pace of life that felt, in many ways, like home.

From strangers to a community

For years, Filipinos in Bali found one another the way migrants always have — through a friend of a friend, at Sunday Mass, at the staff canteen, or by simply recognizing a familiar accent in a crowd. Those informal networks were the real beginning of the community.

Social media changed the scale of it. A dedicated Facebook group gave kababayans a single place to ask questions, trade job leads, buy and sell, look for housing, and — most importantly — organize real-world gatherings. The Filipinos in Bali (BaliFILs) grew out of that need: a way for a scattered, busy, hardworking group of people to find each other and look after one another.

This is a very Filipino instinct. Across Indonesia, Filipinos have formed at least eight community associations, including sports teams, a teachers' association, and Christian fellowship groups. BaliFILs is Bali's own expression of that same instinct — the conviction that no kababayan should have to face life abroad alone.

📣 Help us tell our story

This history is a living document. If you were part of BaliFILs' early days — the first meet-ups, the first Independence Day celebration, the first group chat — we'd love your memories and photos. Share them with BaliFILs admin team so future kababayans know how it all began.

The community today

On Sunday, May 24, 2026, the community gathered in Muntig Siokan, Sanur, for its 2026 Heritage Month celebration and the appointment of a new set of officers — a milestone moment. With a newly appointed leadership team, a clearer structure, and now a dedicated website, BaliFILs is entering a new chapter.

And the vision reaches beyond the island. BaliFILs' long-term goal is to grow from a Bali community into a network that supports Filipinos across all of Indonesia. It started, as these things always do, with a few kababayans deciding to take care of each other. The rest of the story is still being written — by you.

Timeline: Filipinos in Bali & Indonesia

1949

The Philippines and Indonesia establish diplomatic relations on November 24.

1951

The two countries sign a Treaty of Friendship in Jakarta on June 21.

1950s–60s

The first waves of Filipino contract workers reach Indonesia — musicians and artists across Asia, loggers in Kalimantan.

1980s–2000s

Bali's tourism boom draws Filipino hospitality, spa, music, and dive workers to the island.

2010s

Social media connects Filipinos across Bali; the Filipinos in Bali takes shape as an organized group.

2024

The Philippines and Indonesia celebrate 75 years of friendship.

2026

BaliFILs holds its Heritage Month celebration and welcomes a newly appointed set of officers on May 24, and launches its community website.

Cost of Living in Bali: A Practical Filipino Budget

Filipinos in Bali • Published May 25, 2026 • Prices current as of May 2026

"How much do I really need to live in Bali?" It is the first question every newcomer asks. The honest answer: it depends entirely on how you live. Here is a realistic breakdown to help you plan — with sample budgets for a single worker, a couple, and a family.

💱 A note on currency

All figures are in Indonesian Rupiah (IDR), with rough US dollar equivalents using an approximate rate of US$1 ≈ Rp 16,000 (May 2026). Exchange rates move — always check the current rate.

The big picture

Bali is famous for being affordable, and it can be — but "affordable" covers a wide range. A comfortable lifestyle in Bali costs roughly 50–65% less than equivalent living in Western Europe. For Filipinos working in hospitality, there is an extra advantage: many resort jobs include staff housing and meals, which can cut your personal spending dramatically. Always ask what a job package includes.

Cost breakdown by category

Accommodation

This is your biggest variable. Options range from a simple kos (boarding room) for around Rp 1.5–3.5 million a month, to a basic one-bedroom rental, to a private villa. In quieter areas like Sanur, a one-bedroom typically runs Rp 5–10 million (~$315–630) per month. Trendy areas like Canggu and Seminyak cost noticeably more. Long-term leases (6–12 months paid upfront) are usually far cheaper per month than monthly rates.

Food

Food is where Bali becomes genuinely cheap — if you eat local. A hearty meal at a warung costs around Rp 25,000–50,000. Cooking at home with market produce is cheaper still. Eat at Western cafés and restaurants daily and the bill climbs fast. Imported items — wine, spirits, imported beer, Western groceries — cost 1.5 to 3 times more than back home.

Transport

Most people get around by scooter. Renting one costs roughly Rp 700,000–1,200,000 a month; buying a used one is often smarter for a long stay. Petrol is cheap. Ride-hailing apps (Gojek, Grab) are handy for short trips and food delivery. Always wear a helmet and have a valid licence and insurance.

Utilities, internet & phone

Electricity, water, and Wi-Fi together usually run Rp 500,000–1,500,000 a month depending on air-conditioning use. A local prepaid SIM with generous data is very cheap — often Rp 50,000–150,000 a month.

Health insurance

Not optional. Foreigners cannot use Indonesia's public health system, and a single hospital visit can cost hundreds of dollars. Budget for proper coverage — see our Healthcare in Bali guide.

Sample monthly budgets

  • Single worker — modest living~$900–1,200
  • Single — comfortable lifestyle~$1,800–2,500
  • Couple sharing a home~$1,400–2,500
  • Family of four (with school fees)~$4,000–6,000+

Don't forget these

  • Remittances — sending money home to family in the Philippines should be a planned line in your budget, not an afterthought.
  • Visa & permit costs — renewals and the foreign-worker compensation fee, if not covered by your employer.
  • An emergency fund — aim for at least 1–2 months of expenses set aside. Medical emergencies and sudden trips home happen.
  • Upfront housing costs — long-term rentals often require several months', or a full year's, rent in advance.

Smart ways kababayans save

  • Choose a job package that includes housing and meals where possible.
  • Share a house with trusted kababayans to split rent and utilities.
  • Eat local — warung food is delicious, filling, and a fraction of café prices.
  • Live slightly outside the tourist hotspots; prices drop the further you go.
  • Buy a reliable second-hand scooter instead of renting long-term.

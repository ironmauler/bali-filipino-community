Renting in Bali Without Getting Scammed

Filipinos in Bali•Published May 26, 2026

Most rental headaches in Bali are entirely avoidable. They come from rushing, not from bad luck. Take the time to walk through this checklist before you transfer a single rupiah, and you'll save yourself months of stress.

The lay of the land

Most long-term rentals in Bali are paid upfront — often 6 months or a full year in cash. The "monthly rent" you see online is usually divided from a yearly figure. Discount for longer terms; mark-up for shorter ones. A few options:

  • Kos / kost (boarding room) — Rp 1.5–3.5M/month; cheapest, often includes basic furnishing.
  • 1-bedroom rental — Rp 5–10M/month in Sanur; significantly more in Canggu, Seminyak, Ubud central.
  • 2-bedroom villa — Rp 10–25M/month depending on area and pool.
  • Family house — Rp 8–20M/month in residential Denpasar or Nusa Dua.

The 10-point pre-signing checklist

  1. Visit twice, ideally at different times of day. Noise, traffic, smell, and stray-dog density all change.
  2. Check water pressure in every tap and shower. Run both faucets at once.
  3. Test the wifi on-site. Bring your phone, run a speed test, sit in every room.
  4. Verify electricity capacity (in watt). Air-con + kitchen + water heater needs at least 2,200W; 3,500W+ for a comfortable house.
  5. Ask who pays the bills: electricity, water, internet, gardening, pool maintenance, security, banjar fee, garbage. Get the answer in writing.
  6. Inspect for mould — bedrooms, ceilings, under sinks. Bali's humidity is brutal; mould is hard to remove.
  7. Check the area for flooding — visit in the rainy season if possible. Ask neighbours.
  8. Confirm ownership. The person renting to you should be the legal owner or a duly authorised agent. Ask for ID and the property certificate.
  9. Walk the boundary. Some "villas" share a compound; understand exactly what is yours and what is communal.
  10. Photograph everything on the day you move in — every wall, every appliance, every existing scratch. Email the album to your landlord as a record.

The contract: what to insist on

  • A written contract in Indonesian and English (bilingual). No exceptions.
  • Clear start and end dates, and a renewal clause.
  • Itemised costs: rent, deposit, who pays what bills.
  • Deposit terms: deductions only for documented damage; return date specified.
  • Maintenance responsibility: who fixes what (e.g., A/C servicing usually landlord; cleaning usually you).
  • Early-exit clause: how much notice, what is forfeited, what is refunded.
  • Banjar & village fees: included or extra? Often a small monthly fee on top.
  • Subletting: usually prohibited; if you'd like a guest for weeks, get it permitted in writing.

The classic Bali rental scams

⚠️ Watch for these

  • "Pay full year now, no contract." Never. Contract first; payment second.
  • The unverified middleman. "I am the landlord's friend" is a classic line. Insist on meeting the actual owner.
  • Vanishing deposit. "Renovations" mysteriously deducted from your deposit on move-out. Photograph day one; written contract protects you.
  • Double-let. The same property rented to two parties for overlapping periods. Verify ownership and check Google for the listing.
  • "Foreigner price." Real, but smaller than the deposit and bills game.

🇵🇭 A note on property ownership

Foreigners cannot own freehold land in Indonesia. "Nominee structures" (a local holding title for you) are widely used and widely unsafe — many kababayans have lost large sums this way. If anyone offers to "sell you a villa," talk to a licensed Indonesian lawyer first. Rent, don't buy, until you understand the law deeply.

Smart kababayan moves

  • Ask your BaliFILs contacts before signing — chances are someone knows your prospective landlord.
  • Pay 1–3 months first, rather than a whole year, if you can negotiate it.
  • Open a local bank account so rent transfers are clean and traceable.
  • Befriend your kelian banjar; a good neighbourhood relationship is worth more than a slightly cheaper rent.

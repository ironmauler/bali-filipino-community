Schools for Filipino Kids in Bali

Filipinos in Bali•Published May 26, 2026

If you are raising Filipino kids in Bali, schooling is probably your biggest practical question after housing. The island has a wide range of options — and a wide range of price tags. This guide explains the main tiers, the trade-offs, and the questions to ask before you enrol.

The three main tiers

1. International schools

Full English-medium instruction, often with IB (International Baccalaureate), Cambridge, Australian, or American curricula. Expect tuition in the range of US$10,000–25,000+ per year, with separate fees for capital levies, transport, and meals. Well-known schools include Green School Bali (Ubud, IB), Bali Island School (Sanur, IB), Australian Independent School (Sanur, Australian curriculum), Canggu Community School (Cambridge), and Bali International School. Best for families planning to move internationally or back to a Western system later.

2. National-plus / bilingual schools

Indonesian national curriculum with significant English instruction. Tuition typically US$3,000–7,000 per year. Examples include Sekolah Pelangi Kasih, Sekolah Tunas Daud, Sekolah Cikal Bali, and several Catholic schools across Denpasar. A solid middle ground; kids learn Bahasa Indonesia properly while building English.

3. Indonesian national schools

Public and private SD/SMP/SMA. Affordable; instruction in Bahasa Indonesia. Best for long-term integration if your family is committed to Indonesia or your child holds Indonesian citizenship. Some kababayan families choose this for younger years to build language fluency, then transition to bilingual or international for upper grades.

Where the schools are

  • Sanur — Bali Island School, Australian Independent School, Sekolah Pelangi Kasih. Calm, family-friendly area.
  • Canggu / Pererenan — Canggu Community School, Bali International School. Younger expat scene.
  • Ubud — Green School (nature-based, globally known).
  • Denpasar & Nusa Dua — Many national-plus and Catholic schools; convenient for kababayans working in resort areas.

Questions to ask any school

  1. What is your total annual cost — tuition + capital fee + transport + meals + uniforms + activities?
  2. Do you accept mid-year enrolment, and what are placement assessments like?
  3. What support do you give non-native English speakers joining mid-stream?
  4. What is the teacher-to-student ratio and the teacher retention rate?
  5. Do graduates progress to which universities (PH, ASEAN, Australia, US, Europe)?
  6. Is there a sibling discount or scholarship?
  7. What is the refund policy if you transfer your child mid-year?

Filipino-specific notes

  • Documents to bring: child's Philippine birth certificate (PSA + DFA apostille), the past 2–3 years of report cards, transcript of records, and immunisation records — all apostilled if originals.
  • Dependent KITAS: your child stays in Indonesia on a dependent KITAS attached to yours. Most schools require KITAS proof for enrolment.
  • Cultural transition: a good school will support your child through the homesickness phase. Ask explicitly.
  • Faith: Catholic schools exist; some Christian and Muslim schools too. Religious education varies — ask if it matters to your family.

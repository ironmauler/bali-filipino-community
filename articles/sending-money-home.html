Sending Money Home from Bali — A Filipino's Remittance Guide

Filipinos in Bali•Published May 26, 2026

For most kababayans in Bali, remittance is not optional — it's part of the budget, part of the love, and part of the deal we make when we leave home. Here is how to do it cheaply, reliably, and without the small fees that quietly eat 5% of your padala every month.

The three numbers that matter

Every remittance service competes on three things. Look at all three; never just one.

  • Exchange rate margin — most "no fee" services pay you a worse exchange rate than the mid-market (Google) rate. The margin can be 1–4%.
  • Transfer fee — flat or percentage, charged on top.
  • Delivery time — minutes (mobile/wallet), hours (most digital), or 1–3 days (bank wires).

Add the margin and the fee together. That's your true cost. Anything under 1% total is excellent; under 2% is good; over 3% is overpaying.

The options kababayans actually use

Wise (formerly TransferWise)

Generally the cheapest option for IDR → PHP for amounts above a few hundred thousand pesos worth. Uses the real mid-market rate with a small transparent fee. Sends straight to GCash, Maya, or any Philippine bank account. Setup needs your KITAS and a local bank account in Indonesia.

Remitly

Strong consumer experience and good delivery options (GCash, Cebuana, M Lhuillier, Palawan Express, bank accounts). "Economy" rates are competitive; "Express" costs more for instant pickup. Promotional first-transfer rates are very generous.

Western Union & MoneyGram

Still useful for relatives without bank accounts who want cash pickup. Generally more expensive than Wise/Remitly, but unmatched for rural pickup convenience.

BDO / BPI / Metrobank international transfers

If you bank in Indonesia (BCA, Mandiri, BNI) and your family banks at BDO / BPI / Metrobank in the Philippines, direct SWIFT works — but fees and intermediary bank deductions can be surprising. Useful for large one-off transfers, less so for monthly remittance.

GCash (cross-border)

GCash supports inbound international remittance via partners. If your family lives on GCash, this is the smoothest landing for them — and Wise/Remitly both pay into GCash directly.

Quick comparison (typical for monthly padala)

  • Cheapest for digital recipientsWise
  • Best for cash pickup nationwideRemitly Economy / Western Union
  • Fastest to GCashWise or Remitly Express
  • Best for large one-off transfersBank SWIFT (compare quotes)

The five mistakes that cost kababayans money

  1. Looking only at "no fee." The exchange rate margin is the hidden fee. Always compare the final PHP your family will receive.
  2. Sending small amounts often. Flat fees punish small transfers. Batch monthly instead of weekly.
  3. Using money-changer booths to "buy pesos." Two FX conversions = two margins. Send IDR directly.
  4. Forgetting Philippine taxes / charges. Some receiving banks charge incoming-wire fees. Test once with a small amount first.
  5. Skipping the receipt screenshot. If something goes wrong, the transaction reference is everything.

The smart workflow

  • Set a fixed monthly remittance day (e.g., every payday).
  • Compare two services that day; pick the better one.
  • Use the recipient's preferred channel (most kababayan families now prefer GCash).
  • Send a screenshot of the receipt with the reference number.
  • Track your total annual remittance — useful for your own budgeting and for OFW recognition documents.

🇵🇭 You are why this works

The Philippines is one of the world's largest remittance economies. Your monthly padala from Bali is part of that engine. It is also your tangible link to home. Take a few minutes to set it up properly — and you'll save thousands of pesos a year that you can send instead of pay.

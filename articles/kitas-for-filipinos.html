How a Filipino Can Get a KITAS in Indonesia: A Step-by-Step Guide

Filipinos in Bali • Published May 26, 2026 • Information current as of May 2026

If Bali is going to be home for more than a holiday, you need a KITAS. This guide walks Filipinos through the process from start to finish — what to prepare in the Philippines, what your employer handles in Indonesia, what happens after you land, and the mistakes that trip kababayans up. For a wider overview of all Indonesian visa types, see our general Visas & KITAS guide.

The short version

A KITAS (Kartu Izin Tinggal Terbatas — Limited Stay Permit Card) is what legally allows a foreigner to reside in Indonesia for longer than a short visit. The most common one for kababayans is the work KITAS, which is sponsored by an Indonesian employer. The full process — your employer's paperwork plus your visa application and conversion in Indonesia — typically takes 8 to 12 weeks. Good news for Filipinos: the Philippines is not on Indonesia's restricted-nationality list, so you are eligible.

⚠️ Before anything else

Your Philippine passport must be valid for at least 18 months when you apply, and a tourist or visit visa is not permission to work. Renew your passport early, and never start a job on the wrong visa.

Two paths for Filipinos

How you get to your KITAS depends on where you are when the job is offered:

  • Path A — Hired from the Philippines. You go through the formal OFW process via the Department of Migrant Workers (DMW, formerly POEA), apply for your work visa at the Indonesian Embassy in Manila, then convert to KITAS after you arrive.
  • Path B — Hired while already in Indonesia. Your sponsor still files all the Indonesian paperwork, but you usually need to exit briefly (typically to Singapore) to collect the work visa, then re-enter to convert to KITAS.

Either way, the Indonesia-side steps your employer handles are the same: an RPTKA (foreign worker utilization plan), an IMTA (work permit), and the DKP-TKA compensation fee that funds local skills training — commonly billed at around US$100 per month, paid upfront for the visa's full duration.

Path A: Hired from the Philippines

Step 1 — Secure a verified employer offer

Make sure the Indonesian company sponsoring you is real and properly registered (a PMA / foreign-owned company, a representative office, or a local Indonesian company). Ask for the company name, NPWP (tax) number, and address — and verify them. A real employer will also know the KITAS process well.

Step 2 — Register with the DMW

OFWs being hired from the Philippines must process their deployment through the Department of Migrant Workers. Direct-hire requirements, employer accreditation, and your contract verification all flow through DMW. This is also where your Overseas Employment Certificate (OEC) is issued, which you will need at the airport.

Step 3 — Prepare your documents in the Philippines

Get these ready early — chasing them from abroad is painful:

  • Valid Philippine passport (18+ months validity)
  • NBI Clearance (Philippine police clearance)
  • Recent passport-style photos (check the latest size/background rules)
  • Medical exam results from a DMW-accredited clinic
  • Resume / CV
  • Diplomas, transcripts, professional licenses — apostilled by the DFA (the Philippines and Indonesia are both parties to the Apostille Convention)
  • Reference letters from previous employers
  • PSA-issued marriage certificate and birth certificates (apostilled) if you plan to bring a spouse or children later on a dependent KITAS

Step 4 — Your employer files RPTKA + IMTA in Indonesia

In parallel, your sponsor submits the RPTKA to the Ministry of Manpower (Kemnaker) and obtains the IMTA work permit. Once approved, the employer pays the DKP-TKA fee and receives an electronic visa approval (e-visa) for you — the C312 work visa.

Step 5 — Apply for the C312 visa

With the approval reference, you apply for the C312 work visa. Many cases are now handled via Indonesia's e-visa system, but you may still need to coordinate with the Indonesian Embassy in Manila (or the Indonesian Consulate in Davao) for verification or biometrics. Check the embassy's current process before you start.

Step 6 — Get your OEC and fly

Pick up your OEC from DMW so you can clear OFW immigration at NAIA, then travel to Indonesia.

Step 7 — Convert to KITAS within 30 days

After landing on the C312, you have a strict window — usually 30 days — to report to the local Imigrasi (immigration office) and convert your e-visa into a physical KITAS. You'll provide biometrics (photo + fingerprints) and, after a short processing period, collect your KITAS card. A Multiple Exit / Re-entry Permit (MERP) is typically tied to the KITAS so you can leave and return to Indonesia freely.

Path B: Hired while already in Indonesia

The Indonesia-side process is identical — RPTKA, IMTA, e-visa, and KITAS conversion. The difference is the visa pickup: you usually need to exit Indonesia briefly (most commonly to the Indonesian Embassy in Singapore) to be issued the C312, then re-enter and convert to KITAS at Imigrasi within the 30-day window.

👷 If you didn't go through DMW

If you were hired directly in Indonesia and bypassed DMW deployment, please coordinate with the Migrant Workers Office (MWO) at the Philippine Embassy in Jakarta. They can advise on bringing your status into order so you keep your OFW protections.

After your KITAS is issued

Picking up the card isn't quite the finish line. Within the first weeks, also handle:

  • SKTT (Surat Keterangan Tempat Tinggal) — a local residence certificate from the Disdukcapil (civil registry) where you live.
  • NPWP — your Indonesian tax ID, required if you'll be earning taxable income locally.
  • Local bank account — most banks now accept a KITAS for account opening.
  • SIM (Indonesian driver's license) — your Philippine licence can be converted at the local Polres (police station). Useful if you'll ride a scooter, and safer than driving on the PH licence alone.

Who pays for what

  • Employer typically pays: RPTKA, IMTA, DKP-TKA, C312 e-visa approval, and often the Imigrasi conversion fees.
  • You typically pay (in PH): passport renewal, NBI clearance, medical exam, DFA apostille fees, and your own travel costs.
  • Red flag: any "agent" demanding a large placement fee from you for a Bali job. Under Philippine rules, placement fees are heavily restricted, and many such "offers" are scams.

Other KITAS routes Filipinos use

  • Dependent KITAS — for the spouse and children of a KITAS holder, so your family can stay with you.
  • Marriage KITAS — for a Filipino married to an Indonesian citizen.
  • Retirement KITAS — for applicants aged 55+ meeting income or savings requirements (not for working).
  • Investor KITAS — for shareholders in an Indonesian PMA company.
  • Remote Worker visa (E33G) — useful for freelancers and employees of companies outside Indonesia; you live here but earn from abroad.

Renewal & the path to KITAP

Work KITAS validity is typically 6 months, 1 year, or 2 years depending on your role. Renew well before expiry — overstays are charged per day and can affect future applications. After several years of continuous KITAS (or through marriage to an Indonesian citizen), you may qualify for a KITAP (Permanent Stay Permit), which gives you longer-term security.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Starting work on a visit or tourist visa "just for a few months."
  • Skipping DMW registration when hired from the Philippines.
  • Letting your passport drop below 18 months validity mid-process.
  • Missing the 30-day KITAS conversion window after arrival.
  • Using unlicensed "fixers" instead of a reputable, licensed visa agent.
  • Not keeping digital and physical copies of your passport, KITAS, IMTA, and OEC.

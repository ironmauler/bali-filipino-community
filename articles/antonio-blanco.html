Don Antonio Blanco — The Manila-Born Painter Who Made Ubud His Home

Filipinos in Bali•Published May 26, 2026•8 min read

Hero photo: Karinkeren, CC BY-SA 4.0. Illustrative image; not a photo of BaliFILs members.

In a leafy bend of Campuan in Ubud sits a museum unlike any other in Bali — flamboyant, theatrical, completely the work of one man. Its founder was born in Manila, married a Balinese dancer, was honoured by the King of Ubud with the very land it stands on, and lived in Bali for nearly half a century. His name was Don Antonio María Blanco, and he is the closest thing the island has to a famous "Filipino" painter.

From Manila to the world

Antonio Blanco was born on September 15, 1912, in the Ermita district of Manila, in what was then the American-administered Philippines. A small but important nuance: both of his parents were Spanish, so he is more precisely described as Spanish-Filipino — born and raised in Manila, citizen of multiple worlds. He grew up in the Philippines before his life took on the shape of a long, almost mythical voyage.

He studied art in the United States, lived and worked in Florida and California, and developed a deep fascination with the Pacific — inspired by the example of Paul Gauguin and the Mexican artist José Miguel Covarrubias. He set out for Tahiti, but the route took him through Hawaii, Japan, and Cambodia, where he was a guest of Prince Norodom Sihanouk. Then he reached Bali — and stopped.

Arrival: 1952

Blanco landed on the island in 1952 — the year recorded in his Wikipedia biography and on the Blanco Renaissance Museum's official site, the two sources this profile relies on for his Bali chronology. In 1953, he married Ni Ronji, a traditional Balinese dancer who became his life partner, his most painted muse, and his bridge into the deepest layers of Balinese culture. They built a life — and a family — in Ubud, the cultural and artistic heart of Bali.

Recognising his stature, the King of Ubud, Tjokorda Gde Agung Sukawati, granted Blanco the land on which he would build his home, studio, and eventually his museum, in the village of Campuan. It was an extraordinary gift — and an extraordinary public endorsement.

The work

Blanco's paintings were unmistakable: romantic, expressive, dreamy, and almost always centred on women. He painted his wife. He painted his daughters. He painted Balinese dancers and the women of his imagination. His canvases shimmered with theatrical colour and ornate frames he often designed himself. He was prolific, eccentric, and theatrical in person — he received guests at his estate in flamboyant outfits, declaimed poetry, and treated his studio as a stage.

His admirers were numerous and high-profile. Awards and honours followed across the world, including:

  • The Tiffany Fellowship from the Society of Honolulu Artists
  • The Chevalier du Sahametrai of Cambodia
  • Recognition from the Society of Painters of Fine Art Quality under President Sukarno
  • The Prize of the Art Critique in Spain

The Blanco Renaissance Museum

Toward the end of his life, Blanco began transforming his Campuan estate into a permanent home for his work — the Blanco Renaissance Museum. It would house hundreds of his paintings, his frames, his poetry, and the strange, beautiful theatre of his life. He died on December 10, 1999, in Denpasar, Bali, before the museum's full inauguration. His son Mario Blanco, also a painter, completed his father's dream and continues to run the museum today.

The Blanco Renaissance Museum remains one of Ubud's most-visited cultural institutions — listed in Lonely Planet, written up in countless Bali guides, and a fixture of the island's art map.

Three generations of Blancos

The Blanco story did not end in 1999. His only son, Mario Blanco, is an accomplished painter and photographer in his own right — for years quietly stepping out of his famous father's shadow. As President Director of the Blanco Renaissance Museum, he keeps the Campuan estate alive as a working gallery and family home.

The line now runs to a third generation — and straight into the spotlight. Mario's son, Antonio Blanco Jr. (born March 28, 1999, in Bali to Mario and his Balinese wife, I Gusti Agung Ayuwimas Hendrayani), is today one of Indonesia's well-known young actors and models. He broke through with the hit series Kisah untuk Geri (Story for Geri, 2021) and the sinetron Buku Harian Seorang Istri, and earned a Best Actor nomination at the 2023 Bandung Film Festival for the film A+. Off-screen he runs the Kopi Pangeran coffee shops in Denpasar, Seminyak, and Ubud. The grandson of a Manila-born painter is now a household name across Indonesia — the Blanco line woven, three generations on, into Indonesian popular culture itself.

Was he Filipino?

This is the honest question, and it deserves an honest answer. By ancestry, Blanco was Spanish — both parents were Spaniards living in Manila. By birth and upbringing, he was a son of the Philippines: born in Ermita, raised in Manila, formed by the city in which he spent his earliest years. By choice, he became a citizen of Bali — by marriage, by residence, by burial. By legend, he is claimed warmly by Filipinos as one of ours.

For BaliFILs, the simplest framing is this: Don Antonio Blanco was a Manila-born artist who chose Bali, and Bali chose him back. His story belongs to the broader weave of Filipinos — or near-Filipinos, or sons of Manila — who have made the Island of the Gods their home. He is at minimum a fellow traveller; for many of us, a kababayan.

Why he matters to us

The Filipinos in Bali's historical inquiry noted that the earliest sustained Filipino presence in Bali was probably musicians and hospitality workers in the 1960s tourism boom. Blanco arrived earlier than that — 1952 — and quietly settled in Ubud while the rest of Bali was still mostly unknown to the world. Whatever the technicalities of citizenship, he is among the very earliest Manila-born residents of the island. He prefigured the community we are still building.

He also showed something important: that you can be from somewhere else and still be utterly, unmistakably, of Bali. He painted Balinese women. He married a Balinese dancer. He raised Balinese-born children. He died in Bali and is celebrated by Bali. His story is, in its quirky way, a permission slip for every kababayan who has wondered whether they will ever truly belong here. The answer is yes — if you commit to it the way he did.

Visiting the museum

The Blanco Renaissance Museum is in Campuan, Ubud — a short walk from central Ubud, on the road heading west toward Penestanan. It is open daily (check the museum's official channels for current hours and ticket prices). Worth a half-day. Bring your kids, slow down, and let the rooms tell you the story of a man who once decided that Ubud was as far as he needed to go.
